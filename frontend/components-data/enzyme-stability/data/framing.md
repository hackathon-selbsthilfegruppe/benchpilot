## Question

What is the dominant mechanism of enzyme inactivation as pH drops from 7 to 5? The candidates are H1 (His-network disruption), H2 (cofactor displacement), and H3 (aggregation, currently considered ruled out on the strength of Smith 2024). The question is *which*, not *whether* — we already see the activity drop.

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Wild-type and H148A mutant of the target enzyme.
- pH range 4.0–8.0, in 0.5-unit steps.
- Cofactor X — binding, displacement, recovery.
- Steady-state activity assays + spectroscopy.

**Out of scope:**
- Other family members (different enzyme classes).
- Temperatures other than physiological (~25–37 °C).
- Salt / ionic-strength effects beyond standard buffer prep.
- Any irreversible-modification chemistry.

## Success criteria

**Primary:** clear evidence that one of H1 / H2 better explains the data — typically a flatter pH–activity curve in H148A would be decisive support for H1.

**Secondary:**
- Cofactor recovery experiment (EXP-03) cleanly rules H2 in or out.
- Result reproducible on at least two independent enzyme batches.
- Conclusion holds against literature, not in conflict with established findings (Smith 2024 in particular).

**Stretch:** quantitative model of the protonation midpoint that other groups can re-derive from our data.

## Constraints

- **Time:** decide H1 vs H2 by end of Q2 (2026-06-30).
- **Budget:** Q2 envelope €25k. Hard ceiling.
- **Reagents:** H148A mutant has a 3-week lead time and is on the critical path. Cofactor X stock is low; reorder pending.
- **People:** lab capacity is one full-time researcher plus shared instrument time on the plate reader and DLS rig.
- **Hard "don't":** no destructive sampling of the wild-type stock — keep ≥10 mg in reserve for follow-on work after Q2.
